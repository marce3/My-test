Alexandre

Quantitative Analytics

During my final year at university I specialized in financial mathematics, so QA was the obvious next step in my career. I wanted to apply all that I had learnt in my studies - all of the skills I gained from my education - particularly analytical and technical skills.

What happens on the QA graduate programme?

There are two parts - the first is getting together with all the Associates from across the firm to learn about how the business operates. For me the main benefit was meeting a lot of people from many different areas so I built-up my networks. Now if I have a question to ask about a particular business area, I can just pick up the phone and speak to them.

The second part of the training was the quantitative analytics programme. We learnt about rates, credit, FX, commodities, equities, quant trading and so on. I also met with many senior leaders from the different areas so it was very interesting and we learned a lot.

Recently I've been working on a foreign exchange project for the Emerging Markets pegged currencies. I also cover bonds and credit products - so you get very familiar with many different asset classes.

It's possible to move into another business area, some people move into trading or structuring or you can stay on as a Quantitative Analyst.

I like what I am doing right now and I've just been offered a two year secondment to work in our Singapore office. I'm really looking forward to it.

What do you do in QA?

I work in the Emerging Markets Quantitative Analytics team. Our traders deal with a lot of credit, rates and FX products, we bring them tools, or models to price these products and to quantify their risks so we can price them correctly.

In emerging markets there may be a big market move. For example, there could be a big simultaneous move in FX, credit and rates. Traders need to see how the risk will change with this move and understand how the financial models we've created will behave.

We work in three teams - I am in a team covering Eastern Europe, the Middle East and Africa.

What skills do you need in QA?

You need good technical skills, and you need to be passionate about what you do. You need to have perseverance because sometimes it takes a lot of time to get things working. You need to have a creative approach and have good communication skills.

What advice would you give a young graduate starter?

When you are a student you may not understand enough about a job and what the responsibilities are. So you need to talk with as many people as you can learn and understand more. If you like what a quantitative analyst does then go for it.

Johnnie

Money Markets Trading in Treasury

Investment banking is a fast-paced industry where you're surrounded by intelligent people and I respond well to pressure. I studied Economics at university so I developed good quantitative skills which is helpful.

What happens in the graduate programme for Treasury?

We have two graduate programmes within Treasury. One for the trading side of Treasury which lasts for one year. It's two months of initial training before you join the desk where we get an introduction to the bank's products and the industry. This is the programme I joined.

The second training programme is for our governance & risk areas. This is a rotational programme where a graduate will get six months experience in each of the Capital management, Funding & Liquidity Management & Asset / Liability Management areas.

What do you do in Treasury?

The Treasury Execution Services desk is responsible for the funding of the bank. It ensures the bank has funding for all business areas and manages the cash comprising the firm's liquidity buffer.

As in many trading roles it is a challenge to keep on top of all market developments and understand how changes will affect your positions and book. However, as traders within Treasury we have the additional concern of ensuring that our books, which reflect the firms overall cash position, meet various liquidity metrics.

Treasury is a fast developing part of the bank and is becoming more crucial for the industry as a whole. It's really exciting to be working in an area of the bank that is developing so quickly.

What skills are important within Treasury?

You have to be able to multi-task, to work quickly and accurately. It's also important to be analytical and logical as you need to have an awareness of what's going on in the markets and understand the implications that any changes could have on the currencies you manage. For this reason analytical thinking and logic are important traits.

Have you been involved in any Citizenship activities?

In addition to the volunteering that happens during the graduate programme, I've been involved in Barclays citizenship programme. I'm a mentor for Futureversity - a charity that works closely with Barclays to support young adults to achieve the best from themselves.

What advice would you give to a graduate starter?

My advice is keep abreast of market news and keep on top of what is happening with central banks as this is so important in the current environment. We focus on global macroeconomics and understanding how changes in policy and interest rates will form part of the bigger picture, so I'd advise a graduate to develop an outlook and opinion on macro events rather than specific asset classes.
